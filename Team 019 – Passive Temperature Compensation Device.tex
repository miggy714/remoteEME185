\begin{abstract}


Sandia National Laboratories has tasked our senior design team, 
the \teamname, to design a passive temperature compensation device (\device) that regulates gas flow. 
The device must restrict its orifice as the ambient temperature of the surrounding air increases.
The \device\ must be purely thermo-mechanically driven, functioning without reliance on embedded systems (electronics and controls) or user input.
The required mathematical relationship between the ambient temperature and a characteristic orifice geometry, as provided by Sandia Labs,
stipulates that the orifice maintain an effective diameter that varies with temperature between 170 and 380 \textmu m, or half the width of the ballpoint on a pen,
with an average tolerance of $\pm 13$ \textmu m, about the thickness of a human hair.
Our team designed two interconnected subsystems to accomplish this objective:

\begin{itemize}
	\item A square valve orifice, comprising two overlapping square holes
	\item A bimetallic thermal actuator, which expands with rising temperature and drives the valve
\end{itemize}

This Final Design Report illustrates the background, selected architecture, and expected performance of the design and provides a full package required for further development. It achieves the following:

\begin{itemize}
	\item Outlines design motivation, objectives, and architecture
	\item Defines the passive, open-loop control system transfer function
	\item Analytically demonstrates that the required output will be achieved
	\item Identifies likely sources of error
	\item Numerically verifies the predicted system performance
	\item Provides all information needed for further development, including shop drawings, material data, the bill of materials, and vendors for all parts
\end{itemize}

\todo{Report body may not exceed 50 pages.}

\end{abstract}
