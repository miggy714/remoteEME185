\section{Abstract}
\label{sec:abstract}

%--- BEGIN CONTENT ---

% PARGRAPH 1: Project objective and motivation
Sandia National Laboratories (SNL) has tasked our senior design team, 
\teamname\ to design a passive temperature compensation device (\device) that regulates flow in a pipe. 
Flow must be regulated by adjusting an orifice geometry according to a change in ambient air temperature.
The \device\ must be purely thermo-mechanically driven, functioning without the reliance on embedded systems(electronics) or human input.
The required mathematical relationship between the ambient temperature and a characteristic orifice geometry is provided by SNL.
Additionally, materials compatibility with the fluid --primarily noble gases-- must be considered.
In order to meet these requirements, the \device\ will be divided into three main subsystems: (1) \sbsActuator, (2) \sbsInterface, (3) \sbsValve.

% PARAGRAPH 2: Solution and progress to date
This Preliminary Machine Architecture Report outlines how well the current iteration of the three main subsystems conform to the specified mathematical relationship.
Explicitly, the relationship can be summarized as $D_{\text{eff}} = A_1 (T_{\text{ambient}} - T_{\text{ref}}) + A_0$ which will be referred to as the "required relationship" throughout this report.
The \device\ and its constituent subsystems must facilitate this linear relationship between the characteristic orifice diameter and the ambient temperature.

In order to more easily The relationship between the ambient temperature and its corresponding orifice geometry is governed by the following equation:

\begin{equation}
    D_{\text{eff}} = A_1 (T - T_{\text{ref}}) + A_0
    \label{eq: required_relationship}
\end{equation}

Be advised, $A_1 = \num{-1.524e-3} \pm 5\% \frac{\text{mm}}{\degC}$ , $A_0 = 0.254 \pm 5\%$ mm, and
$T-{\text{ref}} = \SI{25}{\celsius}$. The device must operate between \SI{-60}{\celsius}
and \SI{+80}{\celsius}, withstand pressure differentials up to \SI{68.94757}{MPa} during choked flow,
fit within a $(\SI{10}{\centi\meter})^3$ mechanical envelope, and be compatible with hydrogen gas. 

All design concepts were then systematic screened and
weighted with various scoring matrices (Report~1). We have since converged on a single
architecture consisting of three primary subsystems:
(1)~a \textbf{aluminum thermal expansion rod} a singular metallic rod that responds to ambient temperature changes. 
The aluminum rod will have linear piston displacement as a result of thermal exapnsion via the following equation:

\begin{equation}
    \Delta L = L_0\,\Delta T\, \alpha\
\end{equation}

(2)~A \textbf{compound spur-gear train} comprising of two rack gears and two coaxial pinion gears with a gear ratio of
4:1. This amplifies and transmits the piston's linear motion to the valve; and 
(3)~a \textbf{sliding-plate orifice valve} (''pizza cutter'' design), in which two flat plates, one fixed and one translating
contain a shaped hole with the overlap between the two holes defining the effective flow orifice. As the actuator extends with 
increasing temperature, the moving plate translates downwards. This reduces the overlap area and shriking $D_{\text{eff}}$ per 
the requirements.

\subsubsection{Bellows Description and Calculations}
The welded metal bellows transmits an axial stroke $\delta$ from an external 
actuator into a high-pressure hydrogen valve while maintaining zero leakage. 
The bellows must withstand internal pressure $P$, provide the required displacement without plastic deformation, 
and minimize actuator force demand.
\\~\\
Design constraints:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Withstand maximum internal pressure
    \item Operate within allowable compression limits
    \item Maintain acceptable spring force relative to actuator capability
    \item Minimize pressure-induced axial force via geometry
\end{itemize}
Design parameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $P = \text{10,000 psi}$ (maximum internal pressure)
    \item $\delta = \text{0.01053 in}$ (required stroke)
\end{itemize}

INSERT BELLOWS IMAGE HERE
\\~\\
Figure XXX, above, illustrates the valve assembly with the bellows highlighted. Two bellows are used in the design to negate the pressure-induced force, allowing the actuator to focus solely on overcoming spring force and friction. They are welded to the valve body and the valve stem, ensuring a leak-tight seal while allowing for the necessary axial movement. Both bellows are designed to be identical, so the calculations below apply to each bellows independently. The following equations consider important design parameters and constraints for selecting the appropriate bellows geometry and material.
\\~\\
Effective area:
$$A = \pi \left(\frac{D_o + D_i}{4}\right)^2$$
Where $D_o$ is the outside convolution diameter and $D_i$ is the inside convolution diameter of the bellows. The effective area determines the axial force generated by internal pressure. Therefore, minimizing $A$ reduces actuator force demand.
\\~\\
Pressure-induced force:
$$F_P = P A$$
Let $K$ be the spring rate of the bellows, which depends on material properties, geometry, and convolution count $N$. The spring force is proportional to deflection $\delta$.
\\~\\
Spring force:
$$F_s = K \delta$$
Total force required for actuation:
$$F_{total} = P A - P A + 2 K \delta + F_{friction} \pm mg$$
It should be noted that $\delta$ is very small, so the spring force contribution is minimal compared to the pressure force. The two identical and opposing bellows cancel out the pressure-induced force. $F_{friction}$ accounts for any additional forces due to friction in the system, which should be minimized through design and material selection. The term $mg$ accounts for gravitational forces acting on the valve stem.
\\~\\
Let $N$ be the number of convolutions, $D_{max}$ be the maximum axial displacement of the bellows, $L$ be the convolution free length, and $D_c$ be the axial displacement per convolution. The total deflection must be less than the maximum allowed to prevent plastic deformation.
$$\delta < D_{max}$$
Displacement per convolution:
$$D_c = \frac{D_{max}}{N}$$
Increasing $N$ lowers spring rate and increases stroke capacity. Decreasing $N$ increases stiffness and squirm resistance. In this application, $\delta$ is small, so the spring rate is chosen to be low enough to allow for the required stroke while maintaining structural integrity.
\\~\\
When welded metal bellows are subjected to internal pressure, the pressure acts over an effective cross-sectional area and generates a net axial force. This pressure-induced force is independent of stroke and depends solely on system pressure and bellows geometry. In high-pressure hydrogen applications, this force typically dominates the actuator load requirement and therefore becomes a primary design driver.
\\~\\
The pressure required to compress the bellows any distance within its elastic range is given by:
$$P = \frac{K \delta}{A}$$
The maximum operating pressure of the bellows is considered the proof pressure, which is the pressure at which the bellows can be compressed to the required stroke without yielding. Critical squirm pressure is the pressure at which the bellows becomes unstable and experiences lateral deformation. This occurs when the bellows are unrestrained from sideways movement and the internal pressure exceeds the bellows' ability to maintain its shape.
\\~\\
Burst Pressure is the maximum pressure the bellows can withstand while restrained from lateral movement. For this application, the bellows will be restrained from lateral movement by the valve body, so critical squirm pressure is not a primary concern. Therefore, the design is governed by the following relationship:
$$P_{operating} < P_{burst}$$
Where $P_{burst}$ is the maximum pressure the bellows can withstand without failure.
\\~\\
The equations above govern the selection process for the bellows parameters and define the acceptable diameter, wall thickness, convolution count, and material. The parameters of the selected bellows are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Do: in (outside convolution diameter)
    \item Di:in (inside convolution diameter)
    \item t:in (wall thickness)
    \item L:in (convolution free length)
    \item Pburst:psi (burst pressure)
    \item N:(number of convolutions)
